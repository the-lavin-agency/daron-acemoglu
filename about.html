About Daron

The Economist
Redefining Prosperity.

Nobel Prize-winning economist. MIT Institute Professor. One of the most widely cited social scientists in the world. Daron Acemoglu has spent more than three decades building the intellectual frameworks that explain why some societies succeed and others fail — and what must be done, urgently, to shape a better future.

A Life in Ideas

Kamer Daron Acemoğlu was born on September 3, 1967, in Istanbul, Turkey, into an intellectually vibrant household. His father was a lawyer and part-time law lecturer; his mother a teacher. Growing up in a rapidly changing Turkey — politically turbulent, economically uneven, and socially complex — gave Daron an early intuition that the structures of institutions mattered profoundly for human outcomes. That intuition would become his life's work.

He left Turkey to study economics in England, earning his BA from the University of York in 1989 and his MSc and PhD from the London School of Economics, where he completed his doctoral thesis in 1992. The following year, he joined MIT as a lecturer — and has never left. Today he holds the most distinguished academic title the institution bestows: Institute Professor, a rank reserved for faculty of exceptional distinction.

Nobel-Prize Research: Institutions and Prosperity

The work that earned Daron the 2024 Nobel Prize in Economic Sciences — shared with Simon Johnson and James A. Robinson — began in a simple observation: despite roughly similar endowments of geography and natural resources, some countries became dramatically richer than others after European colonialism. Why?

The answer, Daron and his co-authors showed, was institutions. Using historical data on settler mortality rates as an instrument for the quality of colonial institutions established, they demonstrated that extractive colonial institutions produced lasting economic underdevelopment, while inclusive institutions produced lasting growth. The paper — "The Colonial Origins of Comparative Development" (2001) — is among the most cited in the history of economics.

This research program culminated in Why Nations Fail (2012), co-authored with James A. Robinson — a global bestseller translated into over 40 languages that brought this framework to general audiences worldwide. The book argues that inclusive political institutions (those that distribute power broadly and protect rights) enable inclusive economic institutions (those that encourage investment and innovation) — and that this combination, not geography or culture, is the true driver of prosperity.

The Narrow Corridor: Liberty and the State

In The Narrow Corridor: States, Societies, and the Fate of Liberty (2019), Daron and Robinson extended this framework to ask a deeper question: not just how nations become rich, but how they become — and remain — free. Their answer is that liberty requires a delicate, dynamic balance between a capable state and an active, organized civil society. Too much state power produces despotism; too little produces anarchy. The narrow corridor between them is where democracy and freedom can survive — and it is constantly under threat.

AI, Technology, and the Future of Work

In recent years, Daron has become one of the world's most authoritative and clear-eyed voices on artificial intelligence and its consequences for labor, equality, and democracy. His book Power and Progress: Our Thousand-Year Struggle Over Technology and Prosperity (2023), co-authored with Simon Johnson, argues that technological change does not automatically benefit society. Throughout history — from medieval agriculture to the Industrial Revolution — the gains from transformative technologies have been captured by elites, not shared broadly. The same pattern is unfolding today with AI.

His academic work on AI — including "The Simple Macroeconomics of AI" (2024) — provides a rigorous corrective to techno-utopian hype, projecting far more modest near-term economic gains than the industry claims, while identifying the structural conditions under which AI could genuinely improve human welfare. His central argument is that current AI development is too focused on replacing workers and too little focused on empowering them.

This research has made Daron an essential voice in policy debates at the highest levels — from the White House to the World Economic Forum to central banks around the world.

Democracy Under Pressure

In his most recent book, What Happened to Liberal Democracy? (2025), Daron and Robinson return to the most urgent question of our political moment: why are democratic institutions around the world weakening — and what, if anything, can be done to defend and restore them? The book provides both a diagnosis and a prescription, drawing on decades of research into the conditions that make democracy durable.

A Public Intellectual for a Critical Moment

Daron's influence extends well beyond academic journals. He is a regular commentator in The New York Times, The Financial Times, The Economist, and Project Syndicate; a sought-after speaker at Davos, TED, and major policy forums; and a trusted advisor to governments and international organizations. He is the third most-cited economist in the world — remarkable not only for the volume but for the breadth of disciplines his work touches.

He has received honorary doctorates from the University of Utrecht, Bosporus University, the University of Athens, Bilkent University, the University of Bath, École Normale Supérieure, and the London Business School. He is a Fellow of the National Academy of Sciences, the American Philosophical Society, the British Academy, the Turkish Academy of Sciences, the American Academy of Arts and Sciences, the Econometric Society, the European Economic Association, and the Society of Labor Economists.

Daron lives in the Boston area with his family.

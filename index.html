About Daron

Redefining the Economics of Prosperity

Daron Acemoglu is one of the most influential economists alive. His foundational research — on institutions, political economy, labor markets, and now artificial intelligence — has reshaped how we understand why some nations thrive and others fail.

In 2024, he was awarded the Nobel Prize in Economic Sciences (with Simon Johnson and James A. Robinson) for demonstrating that the quality of institutions — not geography, not culture, not resources — is the decisive driver of a nation's long-term prosperity.

AI & the Future of Work — A leading critical voice on how AI is reshaping labor, wages, and democratic institutions.
Institutions & Democracy — Nobel-recognized research on the relationship between political structures and economic growth.
Technology & Inequality — Author of groundbreaking frameworks on how technological choices benefit — or harm — workers.

Areas of Expertise

The Questions That Define Our Era

Daron's research sits at the intersection of economics, political science, and technology — offering frameworks that are both rigorously academic and urgently relevant.

01

AI & Technology

Is AI making us more productive — or is it concentrating power in the hands of a few? Daron's research challenges the techno-optimist consensus and maps a better path forward.

02

Institutions & Prosperity

The Nobel-winning insight: inclusive institutions create inclusive economies. Why some nations succeed and others stagnate, and what can be done about it.

03

Democracy & Liberty

The conditions under which democracy flourishes or declines — and why the balance between state power and individual freedom is more fragile than we think.

04

Labor & Wages

Automation, robots, and the future of work. What happens to workers when technology replaces rather than complements human capability — and how to reverse course.

05

Economic Growth

Thirty years of leading scholarship on the deep roots of long-run development — from colonial history to modern policy design and innovation theory.

06

Inequality

The political and economic forces driving inequality — and why addressing it requires structural reforms, not just redistributive policy.
